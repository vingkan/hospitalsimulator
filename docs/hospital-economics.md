# US Inpatient Hospital Economics — Complete Reference Notes

## Industry Scale

- Total hospital expenditures: **$1.6T** (2024), ~31% of $5.3T national health spending
- ~6,100 hospitals, ~34.4M admissions (2023)
- Avg hospital OpEx: ~$251M; avg operating margin: ~2.1% (2024, down from 7% in 2019)

---

## Revenue Drivers (descending)

- **Commercial/private insurance: ~37–70% of net patient revenue (~$849B+)** — reimburses 2–3x Medicare; cross-subsidizes government payer shortfalls
- **Medicare: ~25% of hospital spending (~$217B+ FFS)** — pays ~83¢/$1; >$100B underpayments (2024); Medicare + Medicaid = >70% of IP days but <50% of revenue
- **Medicaid: ~19%** — pays ~88¢/$1; supplemented by DSH/UPL/state-directed payments (~$48B to hospitals)
- **Outpatient revenue: fastest growing** — ~13% YOY vs ~6% inpatient (late 2024)
- **Non-operating revenue** (investments, grants, philanthropy): variable, often margin-decisive for nonprofits

## Cost Drivers (descending)

- **Labor: ~56% (~$1T+ in 2025)** — direct patient care ~24% OpEx; non-patient-care ~13%; contract labor ~$51B (2023); hospital comp ~$68/hr vs ~$44 private sector
- **Supplies: ~10.5–12% (~$147B+)** — growing ~10–12% YOY
- **Drugs: ~6–9% (~$115B+)** — growing ~13% YOY; avg new drug launch >$370K
- **Admin/billing: ~$43B+ insurer-related** — $18B on overturning denials; overlaps with labor
- **Capital: ~4.8% OpEx** — deferred investment widespread (avg plant age up 10%+)
- **Physician subsidies: ~$317K median/employed physician FTE**
- **Charity care/bad debt: ~1–2%** (highly variable)

## Three Granularity Axes

### Service Lines (strategic lens)

- Revenue/physician: CV surgery ~$3.7M, invasive cardiology ~$3.5M, neurosurgery ~$3.4M, ortho ~$3.3M, family med ~$2.1M
- Standalone profitability misleading — cutting a line destroys downstream ancillary/lab/imaging revenue
- High-margin procedural lines subsidize essential low-margin lines (behavioral health, peds, OB)

### Cost Centers (accounting lens)

- Medicare Cost Report: General Service → Inpatient Routine (med/surg, ICU, OB) → Ancillary (OR, radiology, lab, pharmacy) → Outpatient (clinics, ED)
- Overhead = 43–45% of total costs, allocated to revenue-producing depts
- OR: biggest revenue engine; ICU: most expensive/day; ED: often net loser but critical admission feeder

### Payment Mechanism (revenue cycle lens)

- **Inpatient: DRGs** — lump sum/admission; ~750+ categories
- **Outpatient: APCs** — per-service; single visit → multiple payments
- **Professional: CPT codes** — separate physician billing (facility + professional fee)
- **Commercial:** negotiated, typically 2–3x Medicare
- Real insight = intersection: margin of specific DRG × service line × cost center × payer

## Med/Surg and Hospitalists

### Med/Surg

- Where plurality of inpatient days occur; general-purpose floor (not ICU, not specialized)
- Routine cost center: cheaper/day than ICU (nurse ratios 1:4–5 vs 1:1–2) but lower reimbursement
- Revenue = room/board component of DRG + ancillary charges; throughput-dependent
- Cost dominated by nursing labor → supplies → allocated overhead

### Med/Surg as Central Hub (linkages to all departments)

- **Surgery/OR → Med/Surg:** post-op recovery; OR cannot function without downstream med/surg beds; full med/surg = cancelled elective surgeries = catastrophic revenue loss
- **ICU → Med/Surg:** step-down; if no med/surg bed, patients stay in ICU at 3–4x the cost/day
- **ED → Med/Surg:** admission pipeline; no beds = ED boarding = ambulance diversions, walkouts, HCAHPS damage
- **Cardiology → Med/Surg:** cath lab recovery, heart failure management (HRRP-tracked), post-cardiac surgery step-down
- **Oncology → Med/Surg:** surgical oncology recovery, chemo complication admissions
- **Radiology/Lab → Med/Surg:** demand-driven; turnaround time directly affects LOS (delayed CT = delayed decision = extra day)
- **Pharmacy → Med/Surg:** prescribing patterns (generic vs brand, IV-to-oral conversion) directly affect per-case drug costs
- **PT/OT/Case Management → Med/Surg:** discharge readiness; speed of eval directly determines LOS

### Why med/surg throughput is a C-suite obsession

- Med/surg flow constrains revenue-generating capacity of entire hospital
- 10% LOS improvement unlocks surgical volume, reduces ED boarding, prevents diversions
- Service line profitability is partly illusion — cardiology's cath lab margin depends on med/surg bed availability
- Bed management ("bed czar") operates real-time; med/surg almost always the binding constraint

### Hospitalists

- Manage most med/surg patients; essentially universal
- Structurally unprofitable on professional fees — ~91% require hospital subsidy (~$132K+/hospitalist)
- Value through indirect channels:
  - **Documentation/coding:** raises DRG weights; one program: +$1K/case × 3,000 = $3M
  - **LOS reduction:** ~37% reduction in studies; reduces cost/case under DRG
  - **Throughput:** freed beds → new admissions → new DRG payments (one study: 266 cases/yr = $1.3M)
  - **Specialist enablement:** co-manage surgical patients → surgeons do 20% more cases
  - **Readmission reduction, denial management, quality metrics**

## The Inpatient Day Paradox

**More admissions AND shorter stays are both good — not contradictory:**

- **Under DRG (case-rate):** fixed payment/admission regardless of LOS; every extra day = pure cost, zero revenue; incentive: discharge fast, fill bed with new admission carrying its own DRG
- **Under per diem (some commercial):** paid per day; longer stays = more revenue; reverses the incentive
- **Throughput economics:** at high occupancy, unnecessary days block new high-margin admissions; cancelled elective surgeries = catastrophic loss; at low occupancy, throughput argument weakens
- **Cost pattern:** Day 1 most expensive (OR, workup); later days cheaper but still variable cost
- **Optimization:** maximize admissions × minimize days/admission × maximize CMI × maintain high occupancy

## Case Mix Index (CMI)

- Average DRG weight across all discharges; community ~1.3–1.5, academic ~2.0–2.5, specialty surgical ~3.0–5.9
- **Revenue formula:** DRG weight × base rate → CMI is direct multiplier on inpatient revenue
- **Two ways CMI increases:**
  1. Genuinely sicker patients (referral center, aging population)
  2. Documentation/coding improvement — same care, better capture; CDI programs can boost CMI 14–28% in 1–2 years
- DRG weight explains ~37% of cost variability; 63% is within-DRG efficiency
- Cascading effects: commercial negotiations (evidence of complexity), quality metrics (risk adjustment), benchmarking
- Tension: genuine debate over how much national CMI growth = sicker patients vs coding intensity

## Observation vs. Inpatient Status

- Patient can be in hospital bed for days and still be "outpatient on observation"
- **Two-Midnight Rule:** inpatient appropriate when physician expects care spanning ≥2 midnights
- **Inpatient (Part A):** DRG lump sum; **Observation (Part B):** individual outpatient services (APCs); dramatically lower total reimbursement
- MA plans reimbursed just **49% of actual cost** for observation patients (2024)
- MA observation stays 36.9% longer than traditional Medicare (2024, up from 28.6% in 2019)
- Hospitals use observation defensively — risk of retroactive DRG clawback if audit finds admission inappropriate
- 42% of Medicare short-stay hospitalizations were observation
- **Patient impact:** observation days don't count toward 3-day inpatient requirement for Medicare SNF coverage
- **Cascading effects:** CMI dilution (obs patients removed from DRG pool → remaining IP appears sicker), throughput disruption (obs occupies bed at lower revenue), hospitalist documentation burden

## Quality Measures and Financial Impact

### Channel 1: CMS Pay-for-Performance (direct payment)

- **HRRP:** up to 3% penalty on ALL Medicare admissions for excess readmissions; punitive only, no reward
- **VBP:** 2% withheld and redistributed on performance (outcomes, safety, efficiency, HCAHPS); budget-neutral
- **HAC:** 1% penalty on total Medicare FFS payments for bottom quartile (infections, safety indicators)
- Cumulative max ~6%; avg penalty ~0.28%; safety net hospitals disproportionately hit

### Channel 2: Star Ratings / Market Share (indirect, often larger)

- 1-star increase in Overall Hospital Quality Rating ≈ **3.4% more Medicare revenue/discharge**
- 1-star increase in HCAHPS ≈ **8.8% more net patient revenue/discharge**
- Dwarfs direct VBP adjustments; drives consumer choice, network inclusion, employer steering

### Channel 3: Cost of Achieving Quality

- Infection prevention programs, readmission prevention teams, CDI staff, HCAHPS improvement (staffing, facilities, training), data infrastructure

### Channel 4: Commercial Payer Leverage

- Network inclusion/exclusion, quality-based rate adjustments (1–5%), employer steering (Leapfrog grades), value-based shared savings

### Channel 5: Cost of Poor Quality

- Complications extend LOS 7–10 days under fixed DRG = pure cost; "never events" get no additional payment
- Readmissions: cost + HRRP trigger; avoidable LOS days = zero-revenue bed occupation
- Malpractice (experience-rated premiums), workforce (poor-quality hospitals harder to recruit → more contract labor)

## Hospital Classification (11 Axes)

1. **Ownership:** nonprofit (58%) / for-profit (24%) / government (18%)
2. **Size:** CAH (≤25 beds) / small / medium / large / major (500+)
3. **Location:** rural / suburban / urban
4. **Teaching:** AMC / major / minor / non-teaching (GME/IME revenue)
5. **System affiliation:** system (~67%) vs independent
6. **Payer mix:** commercial / Medicare / MA / Medicaid / uninsured shares
7. **MA penetration:** within Medicare, % MA vs traditional
8. **Payment model:** FFS vs value-based (bundled, ACO, capitated)
9. **Market position:** dominant vs competitive (pricing power: 250–350% vs 150–200% of Medicare)
10. **Service line mix:** procedural-heavy vs medical-heavy
11. **Designations/CMI:** trauma, stroke, cancer center → patient channeling

**Best predictors of financial health:** (1) commercial payer share, (2) market position, (3) ownership type, (4) size, (5) CMI

## Seven Archetypal Hospital Profiles

### 1. Major Academic Medical Center

- 500–1,000+ beds; nonprofit; urban; full AMC; CMI 2.0–3.0+; all major designations
- Heavy Medicare/Medicaid, moderate commercial, notable uninsured
- High revenue/case + GME/IME ($50–150M+) + research + philanthropy; also highest cost structure
- Thin margins (0–4%); mission tension: teaching/research/safety net vs sustainability

### 2. Large For-Profit System Hospital

- 200–500 beds; for-profit (HCA, Tenet); urban/suburban Sun Belt; CMI 1.5–2.0
- Commercial-optimized (40–50%+); high-margin service lines; tight operations
- Operating margins 10–15%+; growing ASC portfolios; portfolio optimization via divestitures

### 3. Large Nonprofit Community Health System

- Multi-hospital (5–20+); regional dominant; mix of teaching/non-teaching
- 30–40% commercial, 30–35% Medicare, 15–20% Medicaid
- Cross-subsidization model; margins 2–5%; pursuing value-based care

### 4. Small Rural / Critical Access Hospital

- ≤25 beds (CAH) or 25–100; heavy Medicare (50–60%+); minimal commercial
- CAHs: cost-based reimbursement (101% of costs); revenue base ~$30–50M; fragile
- 193 closures 2005–2024; dependent on supplements, 340B, tax support

### 5. Urban Safety Net / Public Hospital

- Often large; government-owned; heavy Medicaid (30–40%+), high uninsured (15–25%+)
- Negative operating margin on patient care; survives on DSH, government appropriations, grants
- Essential but perpetually dependent on political will

### 6. Specialty Hospital

- 50–300 beds; focused domain (ortho, cardiac, LTAC, rehab, psych)
- Surgical specialty: very high CMI (3.0–5.9), commercial-optimized, very profitable
- General hospitals resent them as cherry-pickers of profitable cases

### 7. Suburban Growth Hospital

- 100–300 beds; system-affiliated; high-growth corridors; CMI 1.3–1.7
- Favorable payer mix (40–55% commercial); often newest/highest-margin facility in system

## Strategic Decisions

### Largely uniform: Medicare/Medicaid participation, EMTALA, core infrastructure, revenue cycle, local wages

### High variation:

1. **Service line portfolio** — invest vs maintain vs exit
2. **Physician alignment** — employ vs contract vs independent (2/3 of EDs outsourced)
3. **Site-of-service strategy** — HOPD vs ASC vs JV ASC (~$50B volume shift projected)
4. **Scale/M&A** — consolidate vs affiliate vs independent vs divest (45.6% of 2025 deals = divestitures)
5. **Payer strategy** — FFS volume vs value-based risk
6. **Capital allocation** — facilities vs tech/AI vs recruitment vs ambulatory vs debt

### Meta-pattern: Cost structures largely uniform; variation overwhelmingly on revenue/positioning side

## High-Acuity Referral Centers

- Receive sickest/most complex transfers; require 24/7 subspecialty coverage + advanced infrastructure + formal designations
- Higher DRG weights (CMI 2.0–2.5+) = larger per-case payments + significant ancillary revenue
- But: expensive standby capacity, higher nurse ratios, disproportionate uncompensated care
- Works at sufficient volume + favorable payer mix; breaks when cost structure exceeds volume
- Referral network is a relationship — alienating feeders destroys transfer volume

## HOPD vs. ASC vs. Freestanding: Site-of-Service Economics

### Five settings for outpatient procedures:

1. **HOPD on-campus** — hospital's own OP surgery/procedure rooms; paid under OPPS (highest rate)
2. **HOPD off-campus (provider-based)** — separate location under hospital's license/provider number; OPPS (but site-neutral reforms eroding this)
3. **Hospital-owned ASC** — separate Medicare-certified entity; ASC fee schedule (lower)
4. **Joint-venture ASC** — hospital + physicians co-own; ASC fee schedule; hospital captures ownership share of profits
5. **Independent/physician-owned ASC or office** — hospital gets nothing; case lost entirely

### Payment differential:

- HOPDs paid ~1.8x ASCs and ~3.6x physician offices on average
- ASCs receive ~53–56% of HOPD rates under Medicare
- Commercial: HOPD facility fees 2.5–3.5x higher than other settings
- Example: diagnostic colonoscopy — $710 HOPD vs $370 ASC (52% of HOPD)
- But HOPDs also cost more to operate — 42% higher total costs than ASCs for comparable procedures

### Site-neutral payment reform (major ongoing shift):

- CMS systematically closing HOPD premium: new off-campus HOPDs (post-2015) already paid at physician office rates
- 2026: drug administration at grandfathered off-campus HOPDs cut to 40% of OPPS rate
- Inpatient-only list being phased out: 285 procedures removed, 289 added to ASC list for 2026
- Could save Medicare ~$153B over a decade
- Trajectory clear: HOPD premium shrinking and will continue to shrink
- Commercial payers following CMS lead with site steering, prior auth, narrow networks

### Strategic decision framework:

- **Keep in HOPD when:** complex cases needing hospital backup, excess HOPD capacity, strong commercial leverage, site-neutral hasn't reached the service yet
- **Hospital-owned ASC when:** proactively positioning for site-neutral reform, retaining physician preference, competing with independent ASCs, lower operating cost with comparable margin
- **JV ASC when:** aligning physician incentives (ownership stake), sharing capital/risk, preventing physician flight to competitors; must navigate Stark/Anti-Kickback compliance
- **Do nothing = lose volume by default** — independent ASCs capture commercially insured cases; hospital retains complex/government-payer cases; payer mix deteriorates; death spiral

### Emerging hybrid strategy:

- On-campus HOPD for complex cases needing hospital backup
- Hospital-owned/JV ASCs for bread-and-butter ambulatory procedures
- Off-campus HOPDs (where grandfathered) for services still benefiting from rate differential
- Physician offices for lowest-acuity services
- Core question: **lowest-cost clinically appropriate setting the hospital can own rather than lose**

## HOPD Reimbursement by Payer Type

### Structure: every HOPD visit = facility fee (to hospital) + professional fee (to physician)

### Payment by payer (illustrative, moderately complex outpatient surgery):

| Payer                      | Facility Fee | Total (w/ prof) | vs Medicare |
| -------------------------- | ------------ | --------------- | ----------- |
| Commercial (strong market) | ~$9,000      | ~$10,200        | ~250%       |
| Commercial (competitive)   | ~$5,500      | ~$6,500         | ~160%       |
| Medicare FFS (OPPS)        | ~$3,500      | ~$4,400         | 100%        |
| Medicare Advantage         | ~$3,000      | ~$3,850         | ~88%        |
| Medicaid (typical state)   | ~$2,100      | ~$2,700         | ~60%        |
| Medicaid (low-rate state)  | ~$1,500      | ~$2,000         | ~45%        |

- National avg: commercial ≈ 196% of Medicare FFS (2025 Milliman benchmark); facility component higher
- Commercial facility fees at HOPDs 2.5–3.5x any other payer
- Varies enormously by hospital market power, specific payer, geography

### If procedure costs hospital ~$3,200 regardless of payer:

- Commercial (strong): **~$7,000 margin**
- Commercial (competitive): **~$3,300 margin**
- Medicare FFS: **~$1,200 margin**
- Medicare Advantage: **~$650 margin**
- Medicaid (typical): **~($500) loss**
- Medicaid (low-rate): **~($1,200) loss**

### Why this changes HOPD vs ASC calculus by payer:

- **Commercial:** HOPD premium enormous ($3–6K+ per case over ASC); keep in HOPD if payer allows; if payer steers to ASC, capture in hospital-owned ASC rather than lose
- **Medicare FFS:** HOPD premium ~$1,500 over ASC; worth keeping in HOPD; but site-neutral eroding
- **Medicare Advantage:** smaller HOPD premium + high utilization management friction; may generate better net margin in lower-overhead ASC
- **Medicaid:** HOPD often loses money regardless of setting; question is whether to offer the service at all for this payer (usually yes, for mission/surgeon relationships, cross-subsidized by commercial)

### Fundamental implication:

- Hospital with 45% commercial can sustain profitable HOPD (surplus covers government payer losses)
- Hospital with 15% commercial / 40% Medicaid cannot sustain same HOPD cost structure — math doesn't work
- Low-commercial hospitals may be better off in lower-cost ASC setting where margin improvement comes from cost side
